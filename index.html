Here’s a scenario: Imagine that you go to the doctor’s office and discover that you and your partner are going to have a baby. However, both your and your partner’s families have a strong history of Down's Syndrome, with multiple family members possessing it– there is a high chance that your child will inherit this disease. All of a sudden, your doctor offers you the option of altering your child’s genes so that it will no longer be affected, but at a hefty price. In fact, most families probably couldn’t afford to analyze and alter their baby’s genes. Would you choose to design your child’s genetics?

Assisted reproductive technologies offer prospective parents the ability to select their child’s sex and to determine whether their prospective child will inherit a particular disease or be born with a disability. Although current technology allows only for the selection of embryos that have such traits, parents may soon be able to engineer their child’s genome so that he or she has certain desirable traits, like better immunity to disease or higher intelligence.

It’s important to not only study the science behind these procedures, but to understand the larger implications behind such technology. There are many ethical discussions surrounding where to draw the line when it comes to selecting for traits and, in the near future, engineering the genome itself. Should parents be allowed to select the sex of their child? What about selecting against hereditary disease? What implications would genome engineering have on society? Here, we’ll review the use of biomedical technologies to “design” babies, and hopefully provide the tools needed for you to have informed discussions, respectfully deliberate with peers, and form your own opinions on the topic.
